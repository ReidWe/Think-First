A Call to Syracuse University

The Blank Page First.

It is 11 PM in Bird Library. A student opens a new document, and before testing a single idea, opens ChatGPT. The blank page is never truly confronted.

Academic resistance doesn't stop usage; it criminalizes it.

In one Syracuse CSE class with a strict blanket ban, a professor had to file 37 academic integrity reports in a single semester. Banning AI does not work. But neither does ignoring it.

What happens to creative confidence when an AI tool becomes the default starting point rather than the last resort? Over-reliance on AI can hinder students' ability to internalize the writing process and diminish their capacity for independent problem-solving (Khojasteh et al., 2025). The answer is not prohibition. It is structured guidance.

The Core Issue

The blank page is disappearing, and so is the struggle that makes us learn.

Cognitive Outsourcing

When students use AI instead of working through the struggle of idea generation, they skip the intellectual process that builds creative self-competence. Engaging in intellectual challenges activates the brain's reward system and promotes motivation, but AI shortcuts this process entirely (Hasan, 2025). Students recognize that AI might be doing too much thinking for them; they see that brainstorming becomes easier, but only because AI is doing the brainstorming for them (Habib et al., 2024).

The Pattern in Our Data

Our survey found that 47% of Syracuse respondents introduce AI during prewriting or drafting, exactly when developing critical thinking skills matters most. The two respondents who rated their confidence at 6 out of 10 both reported using AI "always" or "often." Meanwhile, the two respondents who use AI "rarely" scored a 9 and a 10 on their confidence rating. While we cannot establish causation from a sample of 15, the pattern suggests that unstructured, early-stage AI use may be linked to lower creative self-efficacy.

Inconsistent Guidance Makes It Worse

This shift is worsened by inconsistent AI policies across schools. Universities have not yet fully integrated AI literacy into their writing programs, leaving students unprepared to engage with these tools ethically (Khojasteh et al., 2025). At Syracuse, clarity scores vary drastically by college. This decentralized environment creates a void where students default to the path of least cognitive resistance.

What we believe.

◯

Pragmatic Understanding

Use AI effectively and interpret output critically. This means having the expertise to recognize when AI-generated work is overly complex or simply wrong, as often happens in technical fields like microcontroller programming (Zhao et al., 2024).

◎

Safety Understanding

Use AI responsibly and ethically. Students must understand data privacy and the professional risks of uploading proprietary or sensitive information to external AI models (Zhao et al., 2024).

◇

Ethical Disclosure

Transparency is the foundation of literacy. By documenting which program was used and how the response was incorporated into your work, you authorize your usage and ensure your integrity is never in question.

△

Reflective Understanding

Assess AI's impact on your learning. This is the "Forklift in the Gym" principle: if you use a forklift to bring weights to your bench, it is a useful tool. But if you use it to lift the weights for you, you have defeated the purpose of the gym (Zhao et al., 2024).

The Proposal

Think First. Then use AI.

Syracuse University should implement a "Think First" pedagogical framework: an AI-Assisted Ideation Protocol that requires students to conduct their initial brainstorm without AI, then introduce it as a late-stage tool to expand, refine, and polish their own ideas.

01

Blank Page Before Prompt

The protocol mandates unassisted brainstorming and outlining before students introduce AI. By requiring students to confront the blank page first, the university protects original thought and ensures AI functions as a tool subordinate to the student, not vice versa (de Araujo & Schneider, 2025).

02

Transparency, Not Surveillance

Rather than relying on invasive detection software, the framework uses AI collaboration statements where students document their process. This shifts the focus from catching cheaters to fostering genuine academic integrity and metacognitive awareness.

03

Zero Cost, Existing Infrastructure

This requires no financial investment or new software. It integrates into the existing WRT 105 and WRT 205 curriculum, and the Syracuse University Writing Center can train peer consultants to guide students through independent brainstorming exercises.

Why Not Just Ban It?

Prohibiting the technology entirely is not a viable option (Saudé et al., 2024). Students actively resist and inevitably bypass outright bans, as the 37 integrity reports in a single CSE class demonstrate. The Think First protocol shifts when and how students engage with AI, not whether they can.

Saudé et al., 2024

The Research Supports It

AI should act as a complement to, rather than a replacement for, human effort (Hasan, 2025). Students who use AI must do so with mindfulness and recognize how the balance of technology and human intuition is very delicate (Fortino, 2025). The Think First framework codifies this principle into policy.

Demand the blank page.

The stakes extend well beyond WRT 205. If university graduates enter professional life having outsourced their initial thinking to a language model throughout their formative academic years, the implications ripple outward. Higher education has always staked its value on cultivating original thinkers. That mission does not become less urgent because the tools for bypassing original thought have become more sophisticated. It becomes more urgent.

Talk to your professors. Email your dean. Share this page. Ask Syracuse to implement the Think First framework, so every student confronts the blank page before opening a chatbot.
